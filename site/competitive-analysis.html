Executive Summary

What the market looks like, and why the door is wide open.

Brake Checker Pro sits in a niche with no direct DTC competitors. The adjacent landscape is dominated by handheld pre-trip multi-tools ($9 to $30) sold through industrial catalogs, B2B leak-detection equipment ($1,000+, quote-only), and big-retailer trucking storefronts. Every site we reviewed scores 6 to 7 out of 10 on visual design. All functional. None aspirational. None mobile-first. None tell a story.

Brake Checker Pro has a patented, USA-assembled, single-purpose solo brake tester at $79.99. The product is real, the cab-installed photos are excellent, and the audience pain point (DOT out-of-service violations, $500 to $2,000 per event) is severe. A premium, single-product DTC site with modern scroll storytelling, sticky CTA, real driver voice, and ROI math will visually and conversion-wise dominate every competitor in the space.

Patterns of the Top 10%

What the strongest pages all do, and what the entire niche is missing.

01

Cite regulations by code, not by feeling

Brake Tech Tools quotes FMCSA 393.75 (tires), 393.47 (brakes), 393.60 (windshield). J.J. Keller talks "CSA scores" and "out-of-service citations" in product copy. Specificity is credibility in this audience.

Source: Brake Tech Tools, J.J. Keller
02

Lead with the financial pain, not the feature

One DOT out-of-service event runs $500 to $2,000 in lost loads, fines, and downtime. The strongest copy frames the tool as the prevention. At $79.99, the math is "pays for itself ten times over the first prevented ticket." Nobody in the niche is showing this calculation.

Gap exploit: ROI math missing across all competitors
03

Patent and USA-made are baseline credibility

Every player who has either claim leans on it hard. We have both, and the product is genuinely patented. A visual "Patented Design · Assembled in USA" badge belongs above the fold.

Source: Brake Tech Tools, J.J. Keller, Redline Detection
04

Real reviews beat lots of reviews

J.J. Keller has 4 to 6 short customer reviews. They convert. The current Brake Checker site has 2 stock testimonials with template avatar URLs visible. That kills trust on inspection. Better to ship empty and add real ones than to ship fake.

Source: J.J. Keller (real); current site (fake)

Where the niche fails: the gap to dominate

No premium design. No mobile-first thinking. No sticky buy bar. No video demo. No real driver voice. No ROI math. Every one of these is a single afternoon of work, and together they create a visual gap so wide that Brake Checker Pro will look like a different category of company.

Recommended Design Direction

Specific choices, each backed by what the competition is and isn't doing.

Primary Color

Brake-light Red #E11D1D

Keep the client's red. Deepen slightly from #F50C0C for legibility on white. Matches the logo and the actual brake light it tests.

Foundation

Asphalt Black #0F0F10

Deep near-black for dark sections, footer, and high-contrast type. Pairs with the red without competing.

Accent

Safety Amber #FFB800

Hazard amber for highlights, ROI callouts, and badges. Already in the brand. Universally read as caution / safety by drivers.

Display Type

Anton or Bebas Neue

Heavy condensed industrial sans for the hero. Nobody in the niche uses one. Instant differentiation.

Body Type

Inter

Modern, neutral, screen-optimized. Higher screen quality than Jost (the current font) without losing the geometric feel.

Photography

Real cab shots over studio

The client already has cab-installed shots. Competitors only have studio. Lead with proof of real use.

Conversion Lever #1

Sticky bottom buy bar

Price + Buy button always visible on scroll. Zero competitors do this. Single biggest lift for a $79 single-product DTC.

Conversion Lever #2

ROI math callout

"One prevented out-of-service = $500–$2,000 saved. This tool: $79.99." Hero or mid-page. Specific dollar numbers convert.
